What to Grow

The Summer Harvest

Summer is the most productive season in the garden. These crops thrive in warm soil, long days, and the kind of heat that would wilt cool-season plants. Pick your players and commit to them.

Vegetables
Vegetable

Tomatoes

The cornerstone of the summer garden. Indeterminate varieties keep producing all season; determinates give one big flush and stop. Consistent watering matters most — irregular moisture causes blossom end rot and fruit cracking.

Deeper tip: prune the suckers (shoots between the main stem and branches) on indeterminate types weekly. This redirects energy to fruit and improves airflow, reducing fungal disease.
Sun8+ hrs
WaterDeep, 2×/wk
Days to harvest60–85
Vegetable

Peppers

Bell peppers and hot varieties both love heat and are slow starters. Once the heat of summer really kicks in, they explode with fruit. Leave a few on the plant to fully ripen to red, yellow, or orange — sweeter and more nutritious.

Deeper tip: peppers are perennials in warm climates. In cooler zones, a healthy plant can be potted up and overwintered indoors near a sunny window for an early head start next year.
Sun6–8 hrs
WaterModerate
Days to harvest70–90
Vegetable

Cucumbers

Fast-growing vines that love to climb. Train them up a trellis to save space, improve airflow, and keep fruit clean and straight. Harvest frequently — cucumbers left too long turn bitter and signal the plant to slow production.

Deeper tip: bitter flavor often comes from heat stress or inconsistent watering, not the variety. Mulch heavily and water on a fixed schedule to keep cucumbers sweet.
Sun6–8 hrs
WaterHigh
Days to harvest50–70
Vegetable

Zucchini & Summer Squash

Notorious overproducers — one or two plants will keep a family well-fed (and looking for recipes). Harvest when small, around 6–8 inches, for the best flavor and texture. They need plenty of room — plan for a 4 ft spread per plant.

Deeper tip: if flowers form but fruit shrivels and drops, it's usually a pollination issue. Hand-pollinate with a small brush, moving pollen from male flowers (thin stem) to female flowers (tiny fruit at the base).
Sun6+ hrs
WaterModerate
Days to harvest45–60
Vegetable

Sweet Corn

Needs space and numbers. Plant in a block of at least 4×4 rather than long single rows — corn pollinates by wind, and a block ensures every ear fills out completely. Eat or preserve within hours of harvest for peak sweetness, which fades fast.

Deeper tip: corn is a heavy nitrogen feeder. Side-dress with compost or a nitrogen-rich fertilizer when stalks are knee-high and again when tassels appear.
Sun8+ hrs
WaterHigh
Days to harvest65–90
Vegetable

Green Beans

One of the easiest and most forgiving crops in the garden. Bush beans are compact and need no support; pole beans climb but produce over a longer window. Succession plant every 2 weeks through midsummer for a continuous harvest.

Deeper tip: beans fix nitrogen via bacteria in root nodules. After the season, cut plants at soil level rather than pulling them — leave the roots to decompose and feed the next crop.
Sun6+ hrs
WaterModerate
Days to harvest50–60
Vegetable

Eggplant

A heat-lover that thrives where tomatoes and peppers do, but takes longer to get going. Glossy, taut skin signals readiness — dull or wrinkled skin means it's past its prime and seeds inside will be bitter and tough.

Deeper tip: flea beetles love young eggplant leaves. Cover transplants with row cover until plants are established and large enough to tolerate some damage.
Sun6–8 hrs
WaterModerate
Days to harvest65–80
Fruits
Fruit

Strawberries

Peak in early summer. June-bearing varieties give a single concentrated harvest; everbearing types produce smaller flushes through summer and fall. Raised beds and containers work beautifully. Protect ripening fruit from birds with netting.

Deeper tip: strawberry plants send out runners that root and form new plants. Let a few establish each year to replace older, less productive crowns — most plants decline after 3 years.
Sun6–8 hrs
Soil pH5.5–6.5
Days to harvest~30 (established)
Fruit

Blueberries

Slow to establish but incredibly low maintenance once settled. They require acidic soil — amend with elemental sulfur or use a dedicated acidic potting mix in containers. Plant two different varieties for cross-pollination and noticeably larger yields.

Deeper tip: mulch with pine needles or wood chips, which slowly acidify the soil as they break down — a low-effort way to maintain the pH blueberries need long term.
Sun6+ hrs
Soil pH4.5–5.5
Years to harvest2–3
Fruit

Watermelon & Melon

Heat lovers that need 80+ warm days to ripen. Give them room to sprawl — vines can run 6–10 feet, or grow compact varieties on a sturdy trellis with fruit slings for support. Telling ripeness takes practice but gets easier with experience.

Deeper tip: for watermelon, look for a dried, curled tendril near the stem and a creamy yellow (not white) underside — both are more reliable than the classic "thump" test.
Sun8+ hrs
Space6–10 ft vines
Days to harvest80–100
Fruit

Raspberries

Summer-bearing varieties fruit on second-year canes (called floricanes) in June and July, while fall-bearing types fruit on first-year canes later in the season. Plant against a fence or sturdy trellis — canes need support once loaded with fruit.

Deeper tip: prune spent floricanes (the canes that just fruited) right after harvest. This directs the plant's energy into next year's canes and keeps the patch from becoming an impenetrable thicket.
Sun6+ hrs
Soil pH5.5–6.5
Years to harvest2 (floricane types)
The Foundation

Soil, Compost & Amendments

Healthy soil is the single biggest factor in a productive summer garden — more than sun, more than variety choice. Here's what's actually in your soil, how to build a compost pile that works, and what to add when something's missing.

Know Your Soil Type

Sandy

Drains fast and warms up quickly in spring, but nutrients and water wash through before plants can use them. Improve by working in plenty of compost, which acts like a sponge.

Clay

Holds nutrients and water well but drains slowly and compacts easily, suffocating roots. Improve by adding coarse organic matter and avoiding walking on wet beds.

Loam

The goal: a balanced mix of sand, silt, and clay that holds moisture but drains well and crumbles easily in your hand. Most amendment efforts aim to move soil toward loam.

Peaty / High Organic

Dark, spongy, and nutrient-rich, but can hold too much water and may be acidic. Great for blueberries; may need lime for crops that prefer neutral pH.

Common Amendments

AmendmentWhat It DoesUse For
CompostImproves structure, adds slow-release nutrients and beneficial microbesAlmost everything, every season
Aged ManureRich in nitrogen and organic matterHeavy feeders like corn and squash — must be aged 6+ months
Elemental SulfurLowers soil pH (more acidic) over several monthsBlueberries, potatoes
Garden LimeRaises soil pH (less acidic)Soil that's tested too acidic for most vegetables
Bone MealSlow-release phosphorus, supports root and flower developmentTomatoes, peppers, root crops at planting
Worm CastingsGentle, won't burn roots, improves microbial activitySeedlings, container plants

Build a Compost Pile

Layer in browns and greens

Alternate 2–3 inches of "browns" (dried leaves, straw, cardboard) with 2 inches of "greens" (kitchen scraps, fresh grass clippings, coffee grounds). Aim for roughly 3 parts brown to 1 part green by volume.

Keep it moist, not soggy

The pile should feel like a wrung-out sponge. Too dry and decomposition stalls; too wet and it goes anaerobic, turning slimy and smelly.

Turn every 1–2 weeks

Moving outer material to the centre reintroduces oxygen and reignites the microbes doing the actual breakdown — this is the step most home piles skip, and the main reason they stall.

Ready in 6–12 weeks

Finished compost is dark, crumbly, and smells like forest floor. If you can still identify the original ingredients, give it more time and keep turning.

What Not to Compost

Avoid meat, dairy, and oily foods (they attract pests and smell badly while breaking down), diseased plant material (pathogens can survive and reinfect next year's beds), and weeds that have already gone to seed (most home piles don't get hot enough to kill seeds).

Care Guide

Keeping Your Garden Thriving

Summer sun is generous but demanding. Heat, drought, and pests can undo weeks of work fast. These are the four pillars of summer garden care — and why each one matters.

💧

Watering

  • Water deeply 2–3× per week rather than shallowly every day — deep roots reach cooler, moister soil and survive drought stress far better
  • Water at the base, never overhead — wet foliage invites fungal diseases like early blight and powdery mildew
  • Morning watering is best; leaves dry before nightfall
  • Drip irrigation or soaker hoses deliver water exactly where it's needed and can cut water use by roughly half
  • Containers dry out far faster than raised beds — check daily during heat waves
  • Self-watering containers or buried clay "ollas" dramatically reduce watering frequency
🌱

Soil & Feeding

  • Work 2–3 inches of compost into beds before planting; top-dress again mid-season
  • Heavy feeders (tomatoes, corn, peppers) benefit from balanced fertilizer every 2–3 weeks through fruit set
  • Switch to a low-nitrogen, high-potassium feed once fruiting begins to prioritize fruit over leaf growth
  • Mulch 2–3 inches deep with straw, wood chips, or shredded leaves to reduce watering, suppress weeds, and moderate soil temperature
  • Beans and peas are largely self-sufficient on nitrogen — over-fertilizing produces bushy plants with few pods
  • A pH of 6.0–7.0 suits most vegetables; test every spring and amend as needed
☀️

Light & Heat Management

  • Fruiting crops (tomatoes, peppers, corn, cucumbers) need a minimum of 6 hours of direct sun — 8 is ideal
  • Orient tall plants (corn, staked tomatoes) to the north side of beds so they don't shadow shorter neighbors
  • Above 95°F, tomatoes and beans often drop blossoms — this is normal and they'll resume once temperatures drop
  • Shade cloth at 30–50% density draped over vulnerable crops during a heat wave can save the season
  • Light-colored or reflective mulch around fruiting plants can cool soil roots by several degrees
  • Floating row covers protect against both late-spring and early-fall frost, extending the season at both ends
🧺

Harvesting Right

  • Harvest frequently and consistently — overripe fruit left on the plant shifts it from producing into seed-setting mode, slowing growth
  • Pick in the morning when produce is cool and fully hydrated; afternoon-picked fruit wilts faster
  • Use clean, sharp scissors or pruners — tearing creates ragged wounds that invite infection
  • Most vegetables hold flavor best at room temperature short-term; tomatoes especially lose flavor quickly in the fridge
  • Succession-plant fast crops (beans, lettuce, radish) every 2–3 weeks to avoid feast-or-famine harvests
  • In late August, mark your best-performing plants for seed saving before the season ends

Reading Your Plants: Water Signals

Overwatering and underwatering often look identical at first glance — both cause wilting and yellow leaves. Here's how to tell them apart before you make it worse.

💧 Signs of Overwatering

  • Yellowing leaves that feel soft and limp
  • Lower, older leaves yellow and drop first
  • Soil is wet or soggy an inch below the surface
  • Plant wilts despite wet soil — usually root rot
  • White mold or green algae forming on soil surface
  • A musty smell at the soil line

🏜️ Signs of Underwatering

  • Leaf edges turn brown and crispy
  • Wilting that recovers fully after an evening watering
  • Soil visibly pulled away from container edges
  • Pot feels unusually light (dry soil is much lighter)
  • Blossom drop on tomatoes and peppers
  • Bitter flavor developing in cucumbers
Bite-Sized Wisdom

Quick Tips & Tricks

A grab-bag of small habits and tricks that don't need their own section, but make a noticeable difference over a season.

01

Plant a few extra seeds of everything — not every seed germinates, and thinning later is easier than gaps in a row.

02

Label everything as you plant. By July, seedlings of different squash varieties look identical, and memory fades fast.

03

Water the soil, not the plant. A quick splash on leaves evaporates fast and does little for roots — aim the hose at the base.

04

Pinch off the first flowers on young transplants for a week or two — it lets the plant establish roots before fruiting, leading to a stronger plant overall.

05

Rotate where you plant tomatoes, peppers, and eggplant each year — they're all in the nightshade family and share the same soil-borne diseases.

06

Save eggshells and crush them around seedlings — a mild slug deterrent and a slow calcium source as they break down.

07

Use the leaves of harvested plants as instant mulch — chopped and laid around remaining crops, they break down and feed the soil in place.

08

Plant flowers along garden edges, not just in beds — borders catch pollinators on their way in and make the whole space more inviting.

09

If a plant looks stressed after transplanting, resist the urge to fertilize immediately — extra nutrients on stressed roots can do more harm than good. Give it water and time first.

10

Keep a small notebook (or phone note) of what you planted where and when — by the third season, this becomes the most valuable resource in your garden.

11

Harvest herbs in the morning after dew dries but before the heat of the day — essential oil content is highest then, giving the best flavor.

12

Don't compost weeds that have gone to seed — toss them in a separate "weed bucket" that gets emptied away from the garden, not into your compost pile.

Pest & Disease Guide

Know Your Enemy

Healthy soil and diverse planting are your first line of defense. Scout every few days — most problems are manageable if caught early. Lean on organic solutions before anything stronger.

AphidsTiny soft-bodied clusters on new growth, excrete sticky honeydew Tomatoes, peppers, beans, cucumbers Moderate Blast off with a firm water spray each morning. Introduce ladybugs or lacewing larvae. Diluted neem oil as a backup.

Companion Planting

Plants That Help Each Other

Strategic planting partnerships improve yields, deter pests, attract pollinators, and make better use of space. These combinations are tried, tested, and genuinely effective.

🌽🫘🎃

The Three Sisters

One of the oldest and most elegant companion planting systems in the world, developed by Indigenous peoples of North America over thousands of years — three crops in perfect symbiosis.

  • 🌽 Corn — The PoleGrows tall and provides a living trellis for beans to climb. Needs block planting for reliable wind pollination.
  • 🫘 Beans — The FixerClimbing legumes that pull nitrogen from the air and fix it into the soil, feeding their companions naturally all season.
  • 🎃 Squash — The MulchSprawling leaves shade the soil, locking in moisture, cooling roots, and crowding out weeds. The prickly leaf texture also deters some pests.
🍅 Tomatoes+🌿 Basil

Basil is said to repel aphids, whiteflies, and spider mites while potentially improving tomato flavor. Both thrive in identical conditions and make great harvest partners too.

Pest repellent
🥕 Carrots+🧅 Onions

Carrot fly is repelled by the smell of onions, and onion fly is deterred by carrots. Interplanting confuses pests that target large monoculture blocks of a single crop.

Mutual pest deterrence
🥒 Cucumbers+🌸 Nasturtiums

Nasturtiums act as a sacrificial trap crop, luring aphids away from cucumbers. Their bright flowers also attract predatory insects that help control pest populations.

Trap crop
🍓 Strawberries+🧄 Garlic

Garlic planted around strawberry beds deters aphids, spider mites, and certain fungal diseases. It's low-growing enough not to compete for light, and you get two harvests from one bed.

Disease protection
🫑 Peppers+🌼 Marigolds

French marigolds release a chemical from their roots that suppresses certain nematodes in the soil. Above ground, their scent confuses and deters many common garden pests.

Root & pest protection
🥦 Brassicas+🌿 Dill

Dill attracts beneficial wasps that parasitize cabbage worms, one of the brassica family's worst enemies. Let some plants flower fully for maximum effect.

Beneficial insect habitat

When Things Go Wrong

Quick Troubleshooting

Even experienced gardeners run into these every year. Here's what's usually behind the most common summer problems.

🍅 Tomatoes have black, sunken spots on the bottom
This is blossom end rot. It looks like a calcium deficiency but is almost always caused by inconsistent watering, which prevents the plant from moving calcium to the fruit. Fix the watering schedule before reaching for calcium supplements.
🎃 Lots of flowers, but no fruit forming
Most likely a pollination problem, common in squash and cucumbers. Check for both male and female flowers (females have a small bulge at the base) and consider hand-pollinating with a small brush, especially early in the season before pollinators are active.
🌿 Leaves are yellowing from the bottom up
Often a sign of overwatering or nitrogen moving to support new growth at the plant's expense. Check soil moisture first — if it's consistently wet, ease off watering before assuming a nutrient problem.
🥒 Plants wilt badly in the afternoon but look fine in the morning
This is usually normal heat response, not a watering problem — plants lose water faster than roots can replace it during peak heat. Worry only if they haven't recovered by the next morning.
🌽 Corn ears are missing kernels or only partly filled
Almost always poor pollination from planting in rows instead of blocks. Corn is wind-pollinated, and a block of at least 4×4 plants ensures pollen reaches every ear's silks.
🍓 Strawberries are producing lots of leaves but little fruit
Usually too much nitrogen. Cut back on high-nitrogen fertilizer and switch to a bloom-boosting feed with more phosphorus and potassium relative to nitrogen.
After the Harvest

Storing & Preserving the Bounty

Summer gardens produce in waves, and the difference between "abundance" and "waste" is often just knowing how to store what you've picked. Here's how to keep produce at its best, and what to do with the surplus.

🍅

Tomatoes

StoreRoom temperature, stem-side down, out of direct sun. Never refrigerate — cold breaks down the cell walls and ruins both texture and flavor.
SurplusRoast and freeze in batches, or simmer into a basic sauce that freezes well for months.
🥬

Leafy Greens

StoreWash, spin dry thoroughly, and store in a breathable bag in the crisper drawer — excess moisture causes rot within days.
SurplusBlanch and freeze sturdier greens like kale and chard; delicate lettuce doesn't freeze well, so eat it fresh first.
🥒

Cucumbers & Zucchini

StoreRefrigerate in a perforated bag; use within a week, as both lose crispness quickly.
SurplusCucumbers are best pickled or fermented. Zucchini can be shredded and frozen for baking later in the year.
🫑

Peppers

StoreRefrigerate unwashed in a loose bag — peppers keep well for 1–2 weeks, longer than most summer crops.
SurplusRoast, peel, and freeze, or slice raw and freeze flat on a tray for easy use in winter cooking.
🍓

Berries

StoreDon't wash until ready to eat — moisture speeds mold. Store unwashed in the fridge in a single layer.
SurplusFreeze on a tray until solid, then transfer to a bag — they won't clump and are ready for smoothies or baking anytime.
🌿

Herbs

StoreTreat soft herbs (basil, cilantro) like cut flowers — stems in a jar of water on the counter, not the fridge.
SurplusHardy herbs (rosemary, thyme, oregano) air-dry well. Soft herbs are best frozen in oil inside an ice cube tray.

If a single crop is suddenly producing more than you can eat fresh, here's how the main preservation methods compare.

Reliable Varieties to Look For

Variety names to search for at nurseries or seed catalogs — chosen for being productive, disease-resistant, and forgiving for less-than-perfect conditions.

Tomato

'Celebrity' or 'Better Boy'

Disease-resistant determinate/semi-determinate types that produce reliably even in an average season — a forgiving choice for beginners.

Cucumber

'Marketmore 76'

Strong disease resistance and consistent straight fruit, even when conditions aren't perfect. A long-time standard for good reason.

Zucchini

'Costata Romanesco'

Slightly more vigorous and flavorful than standard green zucchini, with ribbed skin that holds up well even when fruit gets a little large.

Pepper

'California Wonder'

The classic bell pepper — widely available, productive, and a dependable baseline for comparing other varieties against.

Bean

'Provider'

An exceptionally reliable bush bean that germinates well even in cooler soil and produces early and consistently.

Squash

'Tromboncino'

A vining summer squash that's far more resistant to squash vine borer than standard zucchini, with a nutty flavor when harvested young.

Shopping Smart

Where to Buy Seeds & Tools for Less

Prices shift constantly by season and region, so rather than chase specific numbers, here's how the main sources compare and where each one tends to win — plus realistic price ranges to help you spot a good deal versus a markup.

🏠

Big Box / Home Improvement Stores

Wide availability of basic tools, soil, and seed racks. Convenient, but seed selection skews toward common hybrid varieties and tools toward mid-grade quality.

Best for: Basic tools (pruners, trowels), bagged soil and compost, and end-of-season clearance on tools and raised bed kits.
🌱

Local Independent Nurseries

Often pricier per item, but stock varieties suited to your specific climate and can offer advice no online retailer can. Transplants are usually healthier and better acclimated.

Best for: Transplants (tomatoes, peppers), perennial fruit (blueberries, raspberries), and locally-adapted variety advice.
📦

Online Seed Companies

Best selection by far for unusual or heirloom varieties, often at lower per-seed cost than racks in stores. Shipping costs can offset savings on small orders.

Best for: Heirloom and specialty varieties, bulk seed for succession planting, and seed-starting supplies.
🔄

Seed Libraries & Swaps

Many libraries, community gardens, and local Facebook groups run seed swaps — often completely free, and seeds tend to be regionally adapted already.

Best for: Zero-cost seeds, rare local varieties, and building a community of fellow gardeners.
🛒

Marketplace / Secondhand

Facebook Marketplace, Craigslist, and yard sales are reliable sources for durable tools (forks, shovels, wheelbarrows) at a fraction of retail — quality often beats new budget tools.

Best for: Durable hand tools, wheelbarrows, tomato cages, and large equipment like tillers.
🏪

Dollar Stores & Discount Chains

Surprisingly solid for small consumables — plant tags, twine, small pots, and basic seed packets of common annuals at low cost, though selection is limited.

Best for: Cheap seed-starting pots, twine, labels, and common flower/herb seed packets.

Ways to Cut Costs Further

🌿

Save Your Own Seed

Tomatoes, beans, and peppers are easy to seed-save from open-pollinated varieties — one good harvest can supply seeds for years.

✂️

Propagate from Cuttings

Tomatoes root readily from suckers in water, and herbs like basil and mint propagate easily — turn one plant into several for free.

🤝

Split Bulk Orders

Seed packets and soil amendments often come in quantities far beyond what one small garden needs — splitting with a neighbor cuts cost per person significantly.

🗑️

Use Free Organic Matter

Fallen leaves, grass clippings, and coffee shop spent grounds are often free for the taking and make excellent compost inputs or mulch.

Note: prices above are general estimates as of mid-2026 and will vary by location, season, and retailer — always compare a couple of sources before a larger purchase.
